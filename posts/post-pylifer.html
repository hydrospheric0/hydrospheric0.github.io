PyLifer: Mapping my life birds with Python

Published: March 25, 2026

PyLifer is a Python implementation of lifeR, developed by Sam Safran (see previous post), that builds weekly lifer-opportunity maps from eBird Status & Trends rasters and your eBird life list. While outputs can be generated at 3, 9, and 27 km scales, the 3 km product is by far the most visually appealing.

The map summarizes how many species from your remaining life list exceed a local occurrence threshold each week. This is then animated to show where lifer potential shifts through the year.

Below are three examples for different regions. It is clear that if I want to significantly increase my US life list, I should visit Texas and Florida, especially in spring. In California, the desert region, especially San Bernardino and Los Angeles counties, appears to be a prime life-list destination. In the Netherlands, the picture is less concentrated, but Zeeland (SW), the Wadden (NE), and Limburg (S) stand out as strong areas for adding life birds.

As the maps show, I still have a lot of opportunities to add lifers in all three regions.

The current adaptation of PyLifer was rewritten in Python. Below is a summary table of the benchmark differences between the new code and the original lifeR code.

Cold-run benchmarks on Linux (48-core, NVMe). Three codebases compared: the original lifeR (R), an optimised lifeR build, and PyLifer (Python). At 3 km the original lifeR OOM’d; the optimised R build completes in 40m 46s at 6.6 GB peak RAM; PyLifer finishes in 2m 43s at 24.4 GB. At every resolution PyLifer is the fastest on wall time.

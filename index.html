About

I am an Earth Scientist who develops spatial tools using Remote Sensing and GIS in an attempt to help solve global to local climate, water, and ecosystem crises. Throughout my career I have worked in a variety of applied research settings on projects related to conservation, water resource planning and management, ecosystem studies (forests and wetlands), national forest fire monitoring, fossil fuel industry impact studies, renewable energy zone planning, and climate change adaptation. I strongly support the development of open spatial data and tools.

Help complete the California Bird Atlas

Published: March 22, 2026

In 2026 an exciting 5-year project has started: the California Bird Atlas. This massive effort aims to produce the first statewide Breeding Bird Atlas. Powered by eBird, users can contribute by signing up for the California Bird Atlas Project.

The CA BBA divides California into 16,527 ‘atlas blocks’ (about atlas blocks)—roughly 3×3 mile squares used to map the state’s breeding birds. Atlas blocks are based on 7.5′ USGS Topo Quads that are broken up into 6 smaller blocks. Each block is named with the name of the topo quad followed by the position of the block within the quad.

A common issue is that it is hard to figure out which blocks are not covered yet or which still have poor coverage. For this purpose I created a small web-based app that shows your location relative to the Atlas grid and provides basic coverage statistics.

Try out the development version of this web-based app which should work in any mobile browser.

Mapping my “Lifer” Bird List with lifeR

Published: March 22, 2026

lifeR is an R workflow developed by Sam Safran that answers a deceptively simple birding question: where and when should I go to see new birds for my life list? Starting with downloading my personal eBird data, the code identifies every US species absent from my life list, then fetches weekly occurrence-probability layers for each from eBird Status & Trends via the ebirdst package. Those layers are stacked with terra, and grid cells are scored by the number of species exceeding a 5 % detection probability for that week — producing 52 weekly snapshots of your potential lifers at 9 km resolution across the contiguous US. The frames are stitched into an animated GIF with magick.

Each person’s map will show their birding fingerprint, reflecting where they’ve spent most of their time birding and what species they’ve seen. My map reflects a Pacific Northwest–heavy life list: the South and Southeast lights up year-round (Texas and Florida), while the Great Plains pulse during the breeding season.

It was quite easy to adapt Sam’s code. My changes to the main code are documented here. Note that the amount of downloaded tile data depends heavily on the chosen resolution (3×3, 9×9, or 27×27 km). The process is quite computationally heavy and requires a decent workstation or cluster to run. Note that the script requires an API key that can be requested on eBird.
